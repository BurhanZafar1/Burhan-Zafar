The Story

I grew up in Lahore, Pakistan — a city of 14 million people where the gap between access and aspiration is visible on every street. Before I ever heard the word "internship," I was already learning what it meant to build something from nothing: at 17, I was President of the largest society at the International School of Lahore, organizing ISL Invictus — the biggest under-18 sports event in the country. We generated $8,000 in revenue and secured fully-funded scholarships for 15 students who otherwise couldn't afford to be there.

"I didn't start thinking about capital markets because I wanted a career in finance. I started because I realized capital is the actual mechanism behind everything I cared about."

When I arrived at Whitman College in Washington state — a liberal arts school with 1,500 students and no business school — most people told me I'd chosen the wrong place for finance or tech ambitions. I disagreed. The small environment forced a breadth that a bigger, more siloed university wouldn't have. I ended up in the Economics & Mathematics double major and in the computer science building running experiments at the same time.

My first summer, I interned at Al Noor Sugar Limited in Pakistan — a real industrial company, not a startup accelerator. I built reporting systems used by the actual finance team. It wasn't glamorous. It taught me that real impact is built on unglamorous, precise, reliable work. I came back and applied everything I learned to a Goldman Sachs endowment project — building a $250M allocation strategy from scratch with professionals at the Private Wealth Management office in Seattle.

Now I'm going deeper on the intersection of real estate, private markets, and data — through a Spring 2026 Private Equity Real Estate Virtual Internship Program with Joanna Marie Lopez. I want to be the person who can read a cap table, build the model, write the memo, and build the tool that automates the next version of the process. All of it.
